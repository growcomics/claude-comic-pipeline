# Staging and Composition Guide
## How to Generate Dynamic, Deep, High-Impact Comic Panels

---

## THE CORE PRINCIPLE — WHY STAGING MATTERS

A panel can have a perfect character, a perfect costume, a perfectly rendered transformation — and still feel dead. The reason is almost always the same: **the figures are arranged flat against the camera plane.**

When two characters stand parallel to the lens, shoulders square, facing each other across a level horizontal line, the brain reads "two cardboard cutouts on a shelf." When the same two characters lean into each other along a diagonal, foreheads angled toward a collision point, the brain reads "tension, about to break." Same characters. Same camera distance. Same costume. The only thing that changed is **where the bodies are staged in space.**

This means flat staging is not neutral. **It is actively bad.** It makes the strongest character render look like a product photo and drains every confrontation, reveal, and group shot of its charge. Staging is as important as the pose and the expression — it is the layer underneath them.

**The one rule that governs everything in this guide: the camera plane is the enemy.** Anything that puts the action on a flat plane parallel to the lens flattens the image. Anything that pushes the action *off* that plane — diagonal intent lines, foreground-to-background depth, varied figure scale, asymmetric placement, shooting through a foreground object — creates dynamism. Every technique below is one way to break the plane.

**Default rule:** Every multi-character panel must declare a staging intent before it is generated. Every solo panel wider than a close-up should declare one too. "Two characters at medium distance" is not a composition — it is the absence of one.

---

## THIS WORKS FOR SINGLE CHARACTERS TOO

The strongest examples are multi-character, because that is where flatness is most obvious — a lineup of figures at the same scale on the same plane is the textbook dead panel. But every principle applies to a solo figure.

For a solo panel, the same questions apply:
- Is the figure dead-center facing the lens like a passport photo, or placed asymmetrically with the frame breathing around her?
- Is there a foreground element to shoot *through*, giving the panel depth layers?
- Is there a clear near-to-far axis, or is everything on one plane?
- Is the lead's mass amplified by what surrounds her — negative space, a low angle, an occluding object — or just floating on a backdrop?

A single muscular woman dead-center against a flat wall tells nothing. The same woman pushed into the lower-right third of the frame, lit by a single shaft of light with the rest of the frame swallowed in dark negative space, low-angle so she towers — that panel has weight before she has even moved.

---

## STAGING SELECTION ORDER

When you sit down to stage a panel, pick the staging from the **situation**, not from habit. The five staging types each serve a different beat. Match them:

**1. Two characters in conflict or charged dialogue → TENSION BLOCK**
**2. Lead revealing dominance over a smaller/weaker figure → DEPTH STAGING**
**3. Three or more characters as a group/squad → TRIANGULAR GROUPING**
**4. Solo hero moment, reveal, or splash → NEGATIVE-SPACE HERO**
**5. Intimate or voyeur-witness framing of the lead → FOREGROUND OCCLUSION**

This is not rigid — a confrontation can be shot with foreground occlusion, a reveal can use negative space. But when in doubt, the situation names the staging. The one thing you must never default to is the flat parade-line lineup, where figures stand in a row at equal scale facing the lens. That is the failure mode this entire guide exists to prevent.

## THE UNIVERSAL STAGING TEMPLATE

All five staging prompts below share the same underlying structure. Use this template to build new versions for any beat.

```
[N] character(s) staged in a [staging-type] composition. The LEAD is
[describe lead + transformation state], the dominant focal subject.
[Secondary/supporting characters if any, with their staging positions.]

STAGING — break the camera plane:
[The specific spatial arrangement — diagonal axis, depth layers, triangle,
negative-space placement, or foreground occlusion. State where each figure
sits in NEAR/MID/FAR depth and at what relative scale.]

The composition's main axis runs [diagonal direction / near-to-far / pyramid
lines]. NO figures arranged parallel to the camera plane at equal scale.
NO flat parade-line lineup.

FOCAL EMPHASIS:
[Which body region / transformation the staging is built to showcase, and how
the staging amplifies it — foreground scale, low angle, framing, etc.]

[Per-figure pose + expression — pull from posing-and-expressions.md]

LIGHTING:
[Directional key + fill that supports the depth — a near/far light contrast,
a rim that separates the lead from the background, a shaft that carves negative
space. Flat even light kills depth even when the staging is correct.]
```

**Key rule when using this template:** depth and dynamism come from *staging*, never from faking it with size hierarchy among equals. If three characters are all at the same transformation tier, you create variety through their positions in space (near/far, high/low, scale-by-distance) — not by drawing one bigger than the others on the same plane. The lead reads as the lead because she is staged *closer, larger by perspective, and more central by intent line* — not because she was simply drawn bigger.

## PROMPT 1 — TENSION BLOCK (two-character confrontation)

Use this for rivalry beats, charged dialogue, pre-fight standoffs, "you don't measure up" reveals — any panel where two characters are locked onto each other. The energy comes from the diagonal intent line between them, not from how close the camera is.

```
Two muscular adult women in a TENSION-BLOCK confrontation, staged to break the
camera plane. The LEAD is in the foreground at three-quarter angle, weight
forward on her lead foot, shoulders thrust toward the other figure. The RIVAL
faces her from the opposite side, also leaning in. Their foreheads are angled
toward a collision point between them; the line connecting their two heads forms
a strong diagonal axis from lower-left to upper-right that is the main
compositional line of the frame.

STAGING — break the camera plane:
The two bodies are NOT parallel to each other and NOT square to the lens. Each
is rotated three-quarter so the diagonal intent line dominates. The lead sits
slightly nearer the camera and larger by perspective; the rival is a half-step
back. There is almost no empty horizontal gap between them — the proximity is
part of the tension. NO level eye-line standoff, NO mirrored parallel poses,
NO flat camera-plane staging.

FOCAL EMPHASIS:
The lead's upper-body mass (broad delts, full strained chest, thick arms)
dominates the foreground. The forward lean pushes her shoulders and bust toward
the rival, making her size the visual argument of the panel — she is physically
crowding the other woman.

POSE + EXPRESSION:
- Lead: jaw set, eyes narrowed and locked on the rival, brows slightly lowered,
  lips pressed thin in a fierce focused line. Fierce intensity, not a smile.
- Rival: chin up holding her ground, eyes hard, a defiant half-smirk, one brow
  raised — refusing to back down despite being crowded.

LIGHTING:
Strong directional key from one side so the diagonal between the two faces is
carved in light and shadow. A rim light separates the lead's silhouette from
the rival behind her. Avoid flat frontal fill — it collapses the depth.
```

## PROMPT 2 — DEPTH STAGING (lead dominance over a secondary figure)

Use this for post-transformation reveals, dominance beats, "look at me now" moments — any panel where the lead's scale needs to read against a smaller, farther figure. The Z-axis does the work.

```
Two adult women staged in a DEPTH composition with strong near-to-far layering.
The LEAD is in the immediate foreground, occupying roughly 55-60% of the frame
height, at three-quarter angle. A SECONDARY character is placed deep in the
background — through a doorway, down a corridor, across the room — rendered much
smaller by perspective, roughly 20% of frame height.

STAGING — break the camera plane:
Three distinct depth layers read clearly: FOREGROUND (the lead, dominant and
sharp), MIDGROUND (architecture / floor / equipment receding), BACKGROUND (the
small secondary figure). The environment's perspective lines (walls, floor seams,
ceiling beams) converge toward a vanishing point behind the secondary figure,
pulling the eye from the huge lead in front to the tiny figure behind. The two
women are NOT on the same plane, NOT at comparable scale. The scale gap between
them IS the point of the panel.

FOCAL EMPHASIS:
The lead's transformed body fills the foreground — her shoulder, arm, and chest
mass are the largest shapes in the frame by a wide margin. Her dominance is
established purely by depth and scale: she is enormous and near, the other woman
is small and far. No size cheating — the secondary figure is normal-scaled, she
just reads tiny because she is genuinely deep in the scene.

POSE + EXPRESSION:
- Lead (foreground): confident, a slow knowing smirk, eyes half-lidded and
  steady, chin slightly raised. Smug satisfaction — she knows exactly how she
  towers over the room. Mid-flex or hand-on-hip, owning the foreground.
- Secondary (background): visibly reacting — head tilted up, eyes wide, body
  small and slightly recoiled, awed or intimidated by the figure in front.

LIGHTING:
Warm key on the foreground lead, cooler and dimmer tone in the deep background,
so the depth is reinforced by a near-warm / far-cool light gradient. A rim on
the lead's near shoulder pops her off the receding midground.
```

## PROMPT 3 — TRIANGULAR GROUPING (three or more characters)

Use this for squad shots, crew lineups, group reactions — any panel with three or more characters where the lead must still clearly dominate. This is the cure for the "police lineup" failure mode. Renaissance pyramidal composition: the lead is the apex, the others are the base.

```
Three muscular adult women staged in a TRIANGULAR / PYRAMIDAL grouping. The LEAD
is at the APEX of the triangle — foreground, largest scale in frame, at
three-quarter angle. Two SUPPORTING characters sit at the lower base points of
the triangle, at different mid-depths and different scales from each other.

STAGING — break the camera plane:
The three heads trace a clear triangle: the lead's head high and forward, the two
supporters' heads lower and set back at different distances. NO two figures are
at the same scale or the same Z-depth — one supporter is nearer, one is farther,
the lead is nearest of all. The eye enters at the large lead face and travels
down the pyramid's sides to each supporter. This is the OPPOSITE of a row of
figures lined up parallel to the lens at equal size — there is no flat lineup,
no equal spacing, no level row of heads.

FOCAL EMPHASIS:
The lead's body mass dominates by apex position — closest, largest, highest in
the visual hierarchy. All three women are at the same transformation tier; the
lead reads as the lead purely through staging (foreground + scale-by-distance +
apex placement), not because she is drawn bigger than peers on the same plane.

POSE + EXPRESSION (all distinct — no repeated faces):
- Lead (apex): chin raised, a satisfied closed-lip smile, bright alert eyes.
  Triumphant pride, mid-flex, commanding the front of the frame.
- Supporter A (nearer base point): fierce rallying grin, mouth slightly open as
  if calling out, eyes narrowed with energy, looking up toward the lead.
- Supporter B (farther base point): ecstatic, cheeks high, eyes bright, a big
  open delighted smile, caught up in the group's energy.

LIGHTING:
Key light favors the apex lead so she is the brightest figure; the base
supporters fall slightly into shadow at their greater depths. The light gradient
reinforces the pyramid — brightest and nearest at the apex, dimmer toward the base.
```

## PROMPT 4 — NEGATIVE-SPACE HERO (solo reveal / splash)

Use this for solo hero moments, transformation reveals, chapter splashes, covers — any panel that is about the lead alone at peak. Emptiness around her amplifies her mass.

```
One muscular adult woman at peak transformation, staged as a NEGATIVE-SPACE HERO
composition. She occupies only the lower-right third of the frame. The remaining
two-thirds — upper and left — is dominated by NEGATIVE SPACE: empty sky, void,
dark cavernous architecture, or a single dramatic shaft of light cutting through
otherwise empty space.

STAGING — break the camera plane:
The figure is pushed far off-center, asymmetric, NOT dead-center and NOT square
to the lens. She is shot from a LOW ANGLE so she towers into the empty space
above her, the camera looking up at her from near waist height. The vast empty
region is not wasted — the contrast between her dense, massive body and the
surrounding emptiness is what makes her read as monumental. NO centered
passport-photo framing, NO flat backdrop crowding the figure.

FOCAL EMPHASIS:
Her full transformed silhouette — broad shoulders, full chest, narrow waist,
powerful legs — is carved against the empty space. The low angle foreshortens
and enlarges her, the negative space gives her room to dominate. The emptiness
makes her the only thing the eye can land on.

POSE + EXPRESSION:
Confident hero stance — a double-bicep flex, or hands on hips, or a slow turn
toward the viewer. Expression: a slow confident smirk, eyes half-lidded and
steady, chin raised. Smug satisfaction and total control. She owns the empty
space around her.

LIGHTING:
A single dramatic directional source — a shaft of warm light from above-side
catching her near edge and the dust in the air, the rest of the frame falling to
deep shadow. The light sculpts her mass and defines the negative space. High
contrast, not flat.
```

## PROMPT 5 — FOREGROUND OCCLUSION (intimacy / witness framing)

Use this for intimate beats, voyeur-witness energy, panels where the viewer is positioned as an observer seeing the lead through or past something. The foreground object builds depth and makes the viewer feel present in the scene.

```
One muscular adult woman, the LEAD, staged with FOREGROUND OCCLUSION. The camera
shoots PAST an out-of-focus foreground object — a barbell loaded with plates, a
doorframe, a weight rack, a chain-link fence, an archway — that occupies the
lower-left (or lower-right) 20-25% of the frame as a chunky dark out-of-focus
shape. Through and past that foreground element, the lead is sharp in the
midground.

STAGING — break the camera plane:
Clear depth layers: the soft FOREGROUND object nearest the lens (heavily blurred,
framing one edge), the sharp LEAD in the midground, a soft falling-off BACKGROUND
behind her. The foreground object is not centered or symmetric — it intrudes from
one corner, framing the lead off-axis. This shooting-through-an-object setup makes
the viewer feel positioned in the space, like a witness watching from behind
cover. NO clean unobstructed front-on shot, NO flat single-plane framing.

FOCAL EMPHASIS:
The lead's body is the sharp focal subject, framed and partly bracketed by the
soft foreground object. The shallow depth of field (sharp lead, soft foreground,
soft background) isolates her mass and draws the eye straight to the transformed
body through the foreground gap.

POSE + EXPRESSION:
Mid-action or candid — flexing, adjusting, caught in a private moment of admiring
her own changed body. Expression: eyes directed at her own arm or torso rather
than the viewer, brows raised, a wide private grin. Playful self-admiration, as
if she does not know she is being watched.

LIGHTING:
Shallow-focus cinematic lighting — a soft warm key on the sharp lead, the
foreground object reading as a dark bokeh shape, the background falling to soft
warm tone. The focus and light both isolate the lead in the midground.
```

## APPLYING THESE TO INDIVIDUAL PANELS

When staging a real panel, pull the relevant block above for the beat, then customize:

1. **Pick the staging from the situation** (see Staging Selection Order). Confrontation → tension-block. Reveal → depth-staged. Squad → triangular. Solo hero → negative-space. Intimate → foreground-occlusion.
2. **Drop in the character and transformation state** — the staging blocks are deliberately generic about *who*; the references carry identity (face card, body-tier lineup, costume turnaround). The prompt carries *staging + action + expression* only.
3. **Layer in posing and expression** from `posing-and-expressions.md` — staging is where the bodies sit in space; posing/expression is what each face and body is doing. Both layers are required. A perfectly staged panel with dead faces still fails.
4. **Keep the "break the camera plane" line** in every prompt. It is the load-bearing instruction. The model defaults to flat front-on staging unless explicitly told not to.

Example — a single panel of Vera mid-reveal after absorbing a power-up:
- Staging: NEGATIVE-SPACE HERO (Prompt 4) — solo reveal beat
- Pose: low-angle double-bicep (Prompt 4 hero stance)
- Expression: smug satisfaction (from posing-and-expressions.md mechanics table)
- Focal: full transformed silhouette carved against a shaft of light in empty space

The staging mechanics — figure placement, depth layers, intent lines, scale-by-distance — carry over directly. Always specify them spatially (NEAR/MID/FAR, diagonal axis, apex/base), never just "make it dynamic."

## QUICK REFERENCE — STAGING MECHANICS

Instead of just naming a vibe ("dynamic", "epic", "dramatic"), describe the spatial arrangement physically. These are the moves that break the camera plane:

| Staging move | Mechanical description |
|---|---|
| Tension block | Two figures rotated three-quarter, leaning toward a shared collision point; the line between their heads is a diagonal, not a level horizontal; minimal gap between them |
| Depth staging | Lead foreground at 55-60% frame height, secondary deep background at ~20%; three NEAR/MID/FAR layers; environment perspective lines converge behind the far figure |
| Triangular grouping | Lead head high + forward at the apex; supporters' heads lower and set back at differing depths; no two figures at equal scale or Z-depth; eye travels apex → base |
| Negative-space hero | Figure pushed into one third of the frame; remaining two-thirds empty; low angle so she towers; high-contrast single light source |
| Foreground occlusion | Soft out-of-focus object intrudes from one corner (20-25% of frame); sharp lead in midground seen past it; shallow depth of field isolates her |
| Diagonal intent line | The main compositional line runs corner-to-corner, never level horizontal or dead-vertical; figures' gazes and lean reinforce it |
| Scale-by-distance | Dominance created by placing the lead NEARER the lens, not by drawing her bigger than figures on her own plane |
| Low-angle dominance | Camera below the subject looking up; foreshortens and enlarges; makes the figure tower; reads as power |

| Failure mode (avoid) | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Parade-line lineup | Figures in a row, equal scale, equal depth, all facing the lens — the dead group shot |
| Level standoff | Two figures parallel, square to the lens, level eye-line, empty gap between — the dead confrontation |
| Flat single plane | Everyone on the same Z-depth, no foreground, no background layer — the cardboard-cutout look |
| Centered passport framing | Solo figure dead-center, square to lens, flat backdrop crowding the frame — the dead solo shot |

## ENFORCEMENT (how this stops being optional)

This guide is the *authoring* reference, but a guide you have to remember to read is a guide that gets skipped under throughput pressure. The staging rule is therefore mechanically gated in the per-project QA chain:

- Every multi-character page must ship a `qa/staging/<panel_id>.json` whose top level declares a **`staging_type`** — one of `tension-block`, `depth-staged`, `triangular`, `negative-space-asymmetric`, `foreground-occlusion`, or `parallel-acceptable`.
- `qa/compose.py` **refuses** (defect `D14`) any multi-character page missing a recognized `staging_type`, so a flat panel cannot be composed in the first place.
- For the three depth/tension/triangular types, compose.py also **rejects** flat-camera-plane language in the staging text (e.g. "face the camera", "side by side", "in a row", "lined up", "parallel to the lens", "level eye-line").
- On pass, compose.py **auto-injects** the matching staging directive (the condensed "break the camera plane" sentence for that type) into the composed prompt — so the instruction lands in every submit whether or not the author pasted it.

`parallel-acceptable` is the deliberate escape hatch for formal lineups/portraits; it should be rare.

## PRACTICAL NOTES — generating these on Flow / Nano Banana 2

Field notes from driving these stagings through Google Labs Flow (Omni chat UI, Nano Banana 2), 2026-06-12:

- **Foreground-LEAN beats back-of-head occlusion.** True over-the-shoulder framing — a near character's *back of head / shoulder* large and dark, occluding part of the frame — is the staging the model most often softens back toward a flat two-shot, because it resists hiding a face. The reliable way to get the same depth is a **foreground lean**: the near character angled three-quarter toward the other, *face still visible*, body large by perspective. (Verified: a "she leans in from the foreground, him deeper and smaller, diagonal eye-line" prompt produced a strong diagonal-depth panel on the first try; the matched "over her shoulder, her back fills the foreground" prompt came back milder and flatter.) Reach for lean first; spend true occlusion only when you can afford a re-roll or two.
- **Lock scale with a height-comparison reference, not just prose.** To get Depth Staging's scale-by-distance without the figures drifting to giantess proportions, generate a one-off **height-comparison chart** — both characters standing on a labeled measurement grid at their true heights (e.g. 7'4" vs 6'4") — and attach it as a ref on every multi-character panel alongside the character sheets. Then the prompt only needs the depth instruction plus a one-line clamp: *"their true heights are X and Y; the on-screen size difference comes from camera distance, not from changing their real height."* This pins the ratio (per the height-chart discipline in `lessons-learned.md`) while the staging supplies the drama — far more reliable than describing the gap in words.
- **The Omni agent paraphrases your prompt.** Flow's chat agent rewrites the submitted text before it reaches the model, which can dilute a forceful staging directive. Keep the staging language blunt, imperative, and repeated — lead with the staging type in CAPS and end with an explicit negative ("Do NOT stage them flat / side by side / on a level eye-line"). The condensed directives compose.py auto-injects are written this terse-and-negative way for exactly this reason.
