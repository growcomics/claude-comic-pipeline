# Composition reading list — annotated sources behind L34

L34 (Subject staging and compositional depth — break the camera plane) is a codification of well-trodden territory in cinematography, painting, and comics. This list is the source bibliography. Read in this priority order if you want to deepen the principle.

## Comic-specific

**Wally Wood — "22 Panels That Always Work" (~1980, one-page cheat sheet, public domain by age)**
A single-page diagram every comics artist has seen. Most of the 22 are subject-blocking moves, not camera moves. Direct mappings to L34: panels 7–9 = tension blocking; panel 11 = depth staging; panels 14–18 = triangular grouping. The fastest possible primer on L34 — read first.

**Marcos Mateu-Mestre — Framed Ink (2010) and Framed Perspective vols 1+2 (2016, 2017)**
The modern textbook for graphic-storytelling composition. The Framed Ink chapters on "contrast" and "depth" are the most rigorous treatment of L34's principles in print. Sequel volumes go deeper on perspective + lighting. **Buy these.** The diagrams alone justify the price; many translate directly into our `_l34_staging_directive()` text.

**Will Eisner — Comics and Sequential Art (1985) + Graphic Storytelling and Visual Narrative (1996)**
Eisner invented the modern vocabulary L34 uses — "stress lines," "the line of action," "gesture across panels." The boxer tension-blocking example is straight Eisner. Read both; they're short.

**Scott McCloud — Making Comics (2006), Chapter 1**
"Choices in panel composition." McCloud's five-choice framework (moment / frame / image / word / flow) treats staging as one of the five primary authorial choices, equal to camera selection. Confirms that L34's territory is its own discipline, not a subset of camera work.

**Stuart Immonen, Adam Hughes, David Mazzucchelli — panel-design interviews and lectures**
Mazzucchelli's *City of Glass* and *Asterios Polyp* annotation essays are masterclasses in subject-blocking decisions. Hughes's convention-panel lectures (often recorded) walk through specific composition choices live. Search YouTube + comics-trade interviews.

## Film / cinematography

**Joseph V. Mascelli — The Five C's of Cinematography (1965)**
Composition is the first C. The chapter on actor placement is the canonical source most later directors codified from. Still the textbook for cinematography schools.

**Bruce Block — The Visual Story (2007)**
Explicit treatment of "visual contrast" — Block's term for what L34 calls "breaking the camera plane." Two whole chapters on Z-depth and on linear contrast (parallel vs. diagonal — exactly the L34 tension/static pair).

**Sidney Lumet — Making Movies (1995)**
Chapter "The Look of the Picture" on camera-vs-staging decisions. Lumet's argument: staging IS the look; the camera is just where you observed it from. Confirms L34's claim that subject blocking is upstream of camera placement.

**Tony Zhou — Every Frame a Painting (YouTube, 2014–2016)**
The episode "Akira Kurosawa — Composing Movement" is the single most concentrated demonstration of camera-plane-avoidance available on the internet. The Edgar Wright visual comedy episode covers a related principle. ~15 minutes each. **Watch these.**

**StudioBinder blog + YouTube**
"Cinematography Techniques," "Camera Movement," "The 180° Rule" articles. More applied / less theoretical than Block. Useful diagrams.

## Painting + drawing fundamentals (the deep origin)

**Andrew Loomis — Successful Drawing (1951)**
The chapter on "Spacing and Depth" is the source most modern texts trace back to. One-time read; the rest of the book has lower yield for our use case.

**Renaissance pyramidal composition (Leonardo, Raphael)**
The triangular grouping in our triangular-staging example is the same trick Raphael used in *The School of Athens*. The art-history term is "compositional triangle" or "pyramid composition." Wikipedia entry "Composition (visual arts)" → "Triangular composition" has the lineage.

**Edgar Payne — Composition of Outdoor Painting (1941)**
Payne enumerated ~30 named composition shapes ("Steelyard," "L-form," "Diagonal," "Cruciform"). His ~150-page catalog still drives plein-air painting. Most relevant chapter for the pipeline: "Vista of Depth."

## Concept-art / 3D-CG specific (closest to our target medium)

**Iain McCaig — Visual Storytelling lectures (Schoolism + Brainstorm)**
McCaig (Star Wars Ep I–III, Star Trek 2009) gives the best contemporary lecture on subject blocking for cinematic storytelling. Episode 3 of his Brainstorm course is the closest direct match to our L34 sketches.

**Feng Zhu — FZD School of Design (YouTube)**
The composition + staging episodes are tagged "Design Cinema." The Foreground / Midground / Background episodes apply directly. Higher signal-to-noise than most YouTube concept-art channels.

## Watching/reading priority for a new pipeline operator

If you have one hour: watch Tony Zhou's Kurosawa episode (~10 min), skim Wally Wood's 22 Panels (~5 min), read L34 in lessons-learned.md (~10 min), study the 8 staging-examples in `references/sketches/staging-examples/` (~10 min), browse our own `cinematic-framing.md` "Subject staging" section (~15 min). That's the operator-ready summary.

If you have a weekend: add Mateu-Mestre's Framed Ink chapters on depth and contrast, plus Bruce Block on visual contrast.

If you're going deep: the rest of the list.
